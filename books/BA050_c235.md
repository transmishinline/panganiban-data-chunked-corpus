In cases where the investigation has begun to focus on a suspect, the standard of "individualized suspicion" comes in handy to justify intrusion on an individual's privacy. Yet even if the right to privacy is described as “a broad abstract, and ambiguous” concept (Black, dissenting in Griswold v. Connecticut, 381 US 479, at 509 [1965]), it is, nonetheless, a legitimate one (Ibid.).
