Supreme Court in 1980 held that Chakrabarty's bacterium is patentable. The majority reasoned that Congress meant the patent law to be of wide scope, so as to cover anything under the sun made by man's ingenuity. The fact that the applicant's microorganism is alive is without legal significance for purposes of the patent law, said the majority, citing In re Bergy, 563 F2d 1031.
